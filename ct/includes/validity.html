Argument

  1. A set of statements containing at least one premise and a conclusion.
  2. Conclusion
  3. Premises
  4. Statement

Example 1

Lassie is a dog, and all dogs are mammals. Therefore, Lassie is a mammal.

Example 2

The world was designed by a mind; because clocks have apparent design and were designed by minds, and the world has apparent design.

Example 3

One-third of all traffic accidents involve drunk drivers. So, you are twice as likely to have an accident if youâ€™re sober.

Example 4

The world has a cause, because everything that began to exist has a cause, and the world began to exist.

Finding the Conclusion

All critical thinking students are good students. Rebecca is a critical thinking student.            , Rebecca is a good student.

Finding the Premises

Rebecca is a good student,             all critical thinking students are good students, and Rebecca is a critical thinking student.

Non-Arguments

  • Mere assertions
  • Conditional statements (by themselves)

Deduction and Induction

  • Deductive: intended to be valid.
  • Inductive: probability

Valid

Def.: If the premises are true, then the conclusion must be true.

or

Impossible for the premises to be true and the conclusion false.

Not valid = invalid

Sound

Valid argument with true premises

A valid argument can have

  1. True premises and a true conclusion.
  2. False premises and a true conclusion.
  3. False premises and a false conclusion.
