Argument

  1. A set of statements with at least one premise and a conclusion.
  2. Conclusion
  3. Premises
  4. Statement

Example 1

Lassie is a dog, and all dogs are mammals. Therefore, Lassie is a mammal.

Example 2

The world was designed by a mind; because clocks have apparent design and were designed by minds, and the world has apparent design.

Example 4

Finding the Conclusion

All critical thinking students are good students. Rebecca is a critical thinking student.            , Rebecca is a good student.

Finding the Premises

Rebecca is a good student,             all critical thinking students are good students, and Rebecca is a critical thinking student.

Valid

Def.: If the premises are true, then the conclusion must be true.

or

Impossible for the premises to be true and the conclusion false.

Not valid = invalid

Sound

Valid argument with true premises

A valid argument can have

  1. True premises and a true conclusion.
  2. False premises and a true conclusion.
  3. False premises and a false conclusion.

Valid arguments cannot have true premises and a false conclusion!

Mary will be selected as valedictorian because she has the highest GPA.

Mary was selected as valedictorian because she has the highest GPA.

OBU is consistently one of the highest rated colleges, and it has a very high graduation rate. So, you should consider OBU when choosing a college.

If you study hard, you will do well in this class.

Stop signs are red, because that is a color that stands out and is easily seen.

That post is placed where someone could trip over it. If someone trips, they will get hurt and you will be liable. Therefore, you should paint the post red.

You should do your homework as soon as you can.
