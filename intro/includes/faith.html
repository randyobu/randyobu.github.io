Blaise Pascal: 1623-1662 The Wager: What is his argument? Either God exists or does not Reason does not help us decide for either option. We are forced to choose, which choice is better? Choosing theism costs little, but pays infinitely Choosing atheism costs infinitely, but pays nothing. Dutch book example Rational to believe theism cash out argument in terms of a payoff table. Decisions can be represented using a decision matrix The rows represent your choices. You decide which row is selected. The columns represent various states of the world. You have no control over the columns. What does Pascal's decision matrix look like? | | God Does Not Exist | God Exists | | :---: | :---: | :---: | | **Believe** | -20 | + ∞ | | **Not Believe** | +20 | - ∞ | Choice is to believe or not - equally probable. If you believe, then you adopt a religious lifestyle (a constraint on what you would want to do) If you don't believe, you live any way you want. Expected Utilities: infinity for theism, negative infinity for atheism. Whatever inconveniences are caused by constraining one's lifestyle, they're infinitely outweighed by the threat of eternal punishment. Rational to believe regardless of the evidence. You should choose the option with the best expected outcome. The expected outcome is called the Expected Utility (EU). EU is a function of the payoff, cost, and probability of occurence. * EU(C) = Pr(C) x Payoff + Pr(¬C) x Cost * EU(T) = Pr(T) x ∞ + Pr(¬T) x -20 * EU(A) = Pr(A) x -∞ + Pr(¬A) x 20 ## Objections ### Weak Objections ### 1. God wouldn't reward belief that's based on a bet. 2. Response: the wager is only meant to prove that your belief is rational. 3. The cost of religious belief is not zero. 4. Response: does not change the EU calculation. ### Serious Objections ### * Doxastic Voluntarism: You voluntarily choose your beliefs. * Doxastic voluntarism is false. You cannot choose to believe whatever you want, whether it would be good for you or not. The Wager requires you to have such control. * Response: You can choose to do things that will affect your beliefs. So, you should go to church and pray. ### Strongest Objection ### * The strongest objection to Pascal's Wager is the Many-Gods Problem. * If evidence cannot settle the question of the existence of God, it cannot settle the question of the nature of God either. * What religion does God want you to follow? * How do you select the right religion? Clifford: It is wrong always, everywhere, and for anyone, to believe anything upon insufficient evidence. Pascal's wager fails because there is more than one option. In fact, strong rationalism will also fail for the same reason. For any body of evidence, there is always more than one belief that is consistent with that evidence. That does not mean, however, that I can simply believe anything I like. Some beliefs are better than others, even when they are equally supported by evidence. Several criteria for judging beliefs apart from the evidence have been proposed. Simplicity is one. Simple explanations for the evidence are preferred to complex ones. Internal consistency is another. An important criterion is explanatory power. An explanation that explains much of what I believe to be true is preferred to one that explains little. These criteria, however,
can fail. Sometimes, simple explanations turn out to be false, or our judgments of consistency turn out to be mistaken.
